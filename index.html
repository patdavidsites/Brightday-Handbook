01 — The basics

What is super, and how does it actually work? The explanation to reach for on a first call.

What is superannuation?

[Placeholder] Superannuation is a long-term, tax-effective structure for retirement savings. Money goes in from an employer, from the person themselves, or both — and it's invested and grows over a working life until it can be accessed, generally from preservation age.

How we say it to a client

"Think of super as a tax-advantaged wrapper around your investments — it's not a product itself, it's the environment your money grows in."

How does it work?

Money in

Employer Super Guarantee contributions, salary sacrifice, personal contributions, spouse contributions.

Growth

Contributions are invested according to the member's chosen option — growth, balanced, conservative, or a mix.

Tax treatment

Concessional contributions and earnings are generally taxed at up to 15% inside super — often lower than personal marginal rates.

Money out

Generally accessible from preservation age, subject to a condition of release — see Tab 05.

Investment options, in plain terms

Every investment option is really just a mix of two building blocks — growth assets and defensive assets — in different proportions. The mix determines both the expected long-term return and how much the balance moves around year to year.

Growth assets

Shares (Australian and international), property, and other growth-style assets. Higher expected long-term return, but more volatile — balances can fall as well as rise, sometimes sharply, over shorter periods.

Defensive assets

Cash and fixed interest. Lower expected return, but far more stable — this is what cushions a portfolio against short-term market falls and funds near-term withdrawals.

Risk profiles, from defensive to high conviction

The more growth assets in the mix, the higher the long-term return is expected to be — but the longer a client needs to be able to leave it invested to ride out the ups and downs. As a rule of thumb, the minimum suggested timeframe lengthens as the growth allocation rises.

Risk profileGrowth assetsDefensive assetsMinimum suggested timeframe
Defensive15%85%2+ years
Conservative30%70%3+ years
Moderate (balanced conservative)50%50%5+ years
Balanced70%30%6+ years
Growth85%15%7+ years
High growth90%10%10+ years
High conviction98%2%12+ years
The pattern to explain to a client: as the growth allocation climbs toward 90–98%, the option is built almost entirely around riding out volatility for a materially higher long-term return — which only makes sense with a long enough runway left before the money is needed.
How we say it to a client

"The name of the option matters less than the mix behind it. What we're really matching is how many years this money has left to work before you need it — the longer that runway, the more growth exposure generally makes sense."

The percentages and timeframes above are indicative, industry-typical figures for illustrating the concept — actual asset allocations, option names, and minimum timeframes vary by fund and product. Confirm the exact figures against the relevant PDS or investment menu before using them with a client.

02 — Contributions

The different ways money can go into super, and who each one tends to suit.

Concessional (before-tax) contributions

[Placeholder] Employer Super Guarantee, salary sacrifice, and personal deductible contributions. Taxed at up to 15% going in, counts toward the concessional cap — see Tab 04 for the current figure.

Who this suits

[Placeholder] Clients on higher marginal tax rates looking to reduce taxable income while building retirement savings.

Non-concessional (after-tax) contributions

[Placeholder] Personal contributions made from after-tax money, no further tax on the way in, counts toward the non-concessional cap.

Who this suits

[Placeholder] Clients with a lump sum — inheritance, property sale, redundancy — wanting to boost super tax-effectively.

Salary sacrifice

An arrangement with an employer to direct part of pre-tax salary into super instead of take-home pay.

Spouse contributions

Contributions made on behalf of a lower-income or non-working spouse, which may attract a tax offset.

Government co-contribution

[Placeholder] A government top-up for eligible lower-income earners who make personal after-tax contributions.

First home super saver

[Placeholder] A scheme allowing eligible voluntary contributions to be later withdrawn to help fund a first home deposit.

Worked examples, by income

The same contribution strategy doesn't suit every client — it largely comes down to their marginal tax rate. Based on 2026–27 tax brackets and a $32,500 concessional cap.

Low income — $45,000

Marginal rate is 15% plus 2% Medicare (17%) — close to the 15% contributions tax, so salary sacrifice saves relatively little. The bigger lever here is the government co-contribution: a personal after-tax contribution can attract up to $500 from the government. Spouse contributions may also be worth checking if a partner earns less.

Medium income — $90,000

Marginal rate is 30% plus 2% Medicare (32%) against 15% contributions tax — a 17 percentage point saving on every dollar salary-sacrificed. This is the classic salary sacrifice sweet spot: strong enough tax benefit to be worth prioritising, without triggering Division 293.

High income — $200,000

Marginal rate is 45% plus 2% Medicare (47%) against 15% contributions tax — a 32 percentage point saving, the largest of the three. Maximising the concessional cap each year is usually the priority. Worth checking total income plus contributions against the $250,000 Division 293 threshold, which adds an extra 15% tax on contributions above it.

How we say it to a client

"The question isn't just 'should I salary sacrifice' — it's how big the gap is between what you'd pay tax at personally and the 15% super pays. The bigger that gap, the harder salary sacrifice works for you."

Marginal tax rates shown include the 2% Medicare levy and are based on 2026–27 resident tax brackets — they exclude offsets such as LITO and don't account for HECS/HELP repayments, which can change the practical benefit. Treat as general education, not a personal tax calculation.

Payday Super — the detail

The biggest structural change to how super gets paid since compulsory super began. Employers must now pay super contributions at the same time as wages — matching the pay cycle, whether that's weekly, fortnightly, or monthly — instead of quarterly.

The 7-day rule

Contributions must reach the employee's super fund within seven business days of each payday. If it's not showing in the fund within that window, something's wrong — a genuinely useful thing to tell a client who wants to check their own employer is compliant.

Why it matters for balances

Super that used to sit with the employer for up to 90 days before reaching the fund now gets invested almost immediately — more time in the market means more compounding over a working life, even though the contribution rate itself hasn't changed.

This doesn't change how much goes into anyone's super — it only changes when. The Super Guarantee rate is still 12% either way; Payday Super is purely a timing and compliance reform.

Super on Paid Parental Leave — new this year

For the first time, Government-funded Paid Parental Leave attracts a superannuation contribution. This closes a long-standing gap where parents taking Government PPL — historically disproportionately women — saw their super stall completely during time out of paid work.

Carry-forward (catch-up) concessional contributions

Clients with a total super balance below $500,000 at the end of the previous financial year, and unused concessional cap amounts from up to five prior years, can "carry forward" that unused space and contribute above the standard annual cap in the current year.

Who's eligible

Total super balance under $500,000 as at 30 June of the prior financial year, plus unused concessional cap amounts accrued since the 2018–19 year.

Common use case

A client with a lump sum in a high-income year — redundancy payout, inheritance, or business sale — wanting to catch up on missed contribution years.

Timing matters this year: 2025–26 was the last financial year unused cap amounts from 2020–21 could be used. From 2026–27 onward, any unused 2020–21 cap has expired — the carry-forward window only reaches back five years, so it's worth checking a client's oldest available unused amount before it drops off.

Personal investments — CGT changes

The 2026–27 Federal Budget passed the most significant capital gains tax overhaul since the 50% discount was introduced in 1999. None of it applies yet — the changes land on 1 July 2027 — but clients holding investments outside super need to know it's coming.

Grandfathering is the key detail to get right with a client: any property or asset already held at 7:30pm AEST on 12 May 2026 keeps its current negative gearing treatment. Only established residential properties purchased after that date, and CGT events happening after 1 July 2027, are caught by the new rules.

SMSFs are explicitly excluded from the CGT discount overhaul — a fund's existing effective 10% rate on realised gains in accumulation phase (and 0% in pension phase) is unchanged. See "SMSF and property" below.

New tax — Division 296 (large super balances)

A genuinely new tax, not an adjustment to an existing one. Division 296 is now law and applies from 1 July 2026, targeting individuals — not funds — with large total super balances.

Total super balanceExtra tax on earnings above the threshold
$3 million – $10 millionAdditional 15% (on top of the fund's normal tax)
Above $10 millionA further additional 10% on the portion above $10 million (25% extra in total on that slice)
The final law taxes realised earnings only — interest, dividends, rent, and realised capital gains — not unrealised paper gains, which was the most contentious part of the original proposal. It's a personal tax billed to the individual, not the fund, and it applies whether the client is in an APRA fund or an SMSF.
Transitional rule, 2026–27 only

For this first year, the balance test looks only at 30 June 2027 — a client over $3 million on 1 July 2026 who drops below it by 30 June 2027 won't be caught for this year.

SMSF cost base reset

SMSFs can elect to reset the cost base of assets to market value as at 30 June 2026, so only gains accruing after that date count toward a member's Division 296 earnings.

Both the $3 million and $10 million thresholds are indexed to CPI going forward, in $150,000 and $500,000 increments respectively.

Claiming tax refunds — what's new

From the 2026–27 income year, a new instant $1,000 deduction is available for work-related expenses. An employed client with less than $1,000 in work-related expenses can simply claim the flat $1,000 — no receipts, no itemising. Anyone with genuine expenses above $1,000 can still itemise and claim the higher amount in the usual way, and other deductions (donations, union fees, professional memberships) can still be claimed on top of the instant deduction.

Worth flagging to clients who've never bothered claiming work-related deductions because it felt like too much admin for a small amount — this removes that friction entirely for anyone under the $1,000 threshold.

05 — Accessing your super

The question every client eventually asks: when can I actually get to it? Click through the two steps below for a quick answer, then use the detail beneath for the full picture.

Step 1 — are they working or retired?
Step 2 — what age are they?

Preservation age

Preservation age is 60. The transitional rules that phased it up from 55 fully concluded on 1 July 2024 — everyone reaching preservation age today, and every client going forward, has a preservation age of 60. There's no birth-date table to check any more.

For context only: under the old transitional rules, anyone born before 1 July 1960 had a preservation age of 55, rising in stages to 60 for anyone born on or after 1 July 1964. That transition is now complete and no longer affects any current or future client.

How much access matters, but so does how much tax comes off it. This is the short version — see Tab 10 for the full breakdown of contributions and earnings tax.

From 60 onward, the whole question disappears: it's simply tax-free.
The low rate cap and related thresholds are indexed and can change each financial year — confirm current figures against ato.gov.au before quoting them to a client. This is general information, not personal tax advice.

Minimum and maximum are both recalculated each 1 July based on the account balance on that date (or on a pro-rata basis in the pension's first year, based on the balance at commencement).

The 4% rule — a planning heuristic, not a legal limit

The legislated figures above are compliance requirements. Separately, the "4% rule" is a widely used rough guide for how much a client could sustainably spend, unrelated to the legal minimum or maximum: draw around 4% of the starting balance in year one, then adjust that dollar amount for inflation each year after. It comes from historical (largely US-based) market research suggesting that pattern had a high chance of lasting roughly 30 years.

Worked example

A $1,000,000 balance at retirement → roughly $40,000 in year one. In year two, that $40,000 is simply adjusted for inflation, regardless of how the portfolio performed.

Where it falls short

It's a heuristic, not a promise. It's based on historical market returns and a fixed 30-year horizon, doesn't factor in the Age Pension as a safety net, and ignores a client's actual asset allocation and sequencing risk.

Coincidence worth flagging to a client: the 4% rule figure and the legislated minimum for a retirement phase account under 65 happen to both be 4%. They're not the same thing — one is a spending guideline, the other is a compliance floor — and the legislated minimum keeps climbing with age while the 4% rule doesn't adjust for age at all.
How we say it to a client

"It's not just about turning a certain age — it's about age plus situation. Two clients the same age can have very different answers depending on whether they've actually retired, are still working, or are drawing down through a transition to retirement strategy. And once the money's accessible, how much you draw each year matters just as much as when you can start."

07 — Insurance basics

The three covers that live inside most super funds, explained the way you'd explain them on a first call.

Life cover (death cover)

Pays a lump sum to a client's beneficiaries, or their estate, if they die — and with most policies, if they're diagnosed with a terminal illness and given a limited life expectancy. It's designed to clear debt, replace lost income for dependants, and cover final expenses.

How we say it to a client

"Life cover isn't really for you — it's for the people who rely on your income. The question is always: if you weren't here, would they be okay?"

TPD cover (total and permanent disability)

Pays a lump sum if a client becomes permanently unable to work due to illness or injury. Two common definitions insurers use:

Any occupation

Pays out if the client can't work in any job they're reasonably suited to by education, training or experience — a higher bar to meet. This is the definition generally available inside super.

Own occupation

Pays out if the client can't work in their specific occupation, even if they could do other work — broader protection, particularly for specialised professions. Generally only available outside super — more in Tab 08.

Income protection (salary continuance)

Pays a regular, ongoing income — rather than a lump sum — if a client can't work due to illness or injury, after a chosen waiting period and for a chosen benefit period. It's designed to replace part of a client's take-home pay while they recover, rather than pay out a one-off amount.

Waiting period

How long a client is off work before payments start — commonly a choice between shorter periods (faster payment, higher premium) and longer periods (cheaper premium, more savings needed to bridge the gap).

Benefit period

How long payments continue once they start — commonly a fixed number of years or through to a set age. Longer benefit periods cost more but protect against a longer-term or permanent inability to work.

08 — Insurance in depth

Once a client understands what each cover does, these are the conversations that actually change the outcome: default vs tailored cover, whether it's held in or out of super, and the tax angle on income protection.

Default cover vs tailored cover

Most super funds automatically provide a starting level of life and TPD cover — and sometimes income protection — when an account is opened, without underwriting. It's convenient, but it's built for an average member, not a specific one.

Default cover

Automatic, no underwriting to start, generally cheaper per dollar of cover. Sum insured is usually age-based and often reduces as the client gets older — regardless of whether their actual needs (debt, dependants, income) have gone up. Cover can lapse if an account becomes inactive for an extended period, unless the client opts to keep it.

Tailored cover

Underwritten to the individual, sized to their actual debts, income and dependants rather than a standard scale. Can hold level cover instead of stepped/age-reducing cover, and isn't tied to a single default fund.

Cover that follows a client through career changes

Default cover is attached to whichever super fund a client's employer happens to use. Change jobs, and it's easy for cover to be duplicated, lost, or left behind in an old account without the client realising. Tailored cover, held under the client's own name rather than an employer's default arrangement, moves with them regardless of who they work for.

Portability

Cover stays in force through job changes, career breaks, or moves into self-employment — no re-underwriting triggered just by changing employer.

Guaranteed future insurability

Many tailored policies allow the client to increase cover at defined life events — new mortgage, new child, salary increase — without full medical underwriting each time.

This is a common gap to check for: a client who's changed jobs two or three times may be paying for several small, stacked default policies across old super accounts, none of which is actually sized to their current situation.

The tax angle: income protection outside super

This is one of the more consistently overlooked planning points. Income protection premiums paid personally, outside super, are generally tax-deductible to the individual at their marginal tax rate — because the benefit itself, if a claim is made, is treated as assessable income. Held inside super, the client doesn't get that personal deduction; the fund itself may claim a deduction internally, but it doesn't flow through to the client's own tax return.

In practice: a client paying income protection premiums outside super can often reduce the effective cost of that premium substantially through the tax deduction — while a client paying the same premium from inside super gets none of that personal benefit, even though the premium is quietly reducing their retirement balance.
How we say it to a client

"Income protection is usually the one cover worth pulling out of super and holding personally — you get a tax deduction on the premium, and it stops quietly eating into your retirement balance every year."

Tax treatment depends on each client's individual circumstances, and deductibility of income protection premiums is a tax position, not general product advice — confirm current ATO guidance and, where appropriate, involve the client's tax agent or accountant before this is discussed as a recommendation rather than general information.

10 — Tax on super

Super is taxed differently at three points: while money goes in and grows, once a client can start withdrawing from age 60, and once they're fully in retirement phase. This is the sequence to walk a client through.

1. While it's growing — the accumulation phase

Contributions tax

Concessional contributions (employer SG, salary sacrifice, personal deductible) are taxed at 15% as they go into the fund. High-income earners pay an extra 15% under Division 293 on contributions above the relevant threshold. Non-concessional contributions have no tax on entry — they're already after-tax money.

Earnings tax

Investment earnings inside the fund are taxed at up to 15% while in accumulation phase. Capital gains on assets held longer than 12 months get a one-third discount, bringing the effective rate down to around 10% on those gains.

This 15%-ish tax environment is the core reason super is tax-effective compared to earning and investing the same money personally at a higher marginal rate — see Tab 02 for worked examples by income.

2. From age 60 — withdrawals begin

Once a client turns 60 and meets a condition of release, withdrawals from a taxed super fund — whether a lump sum or an income stream — are completely tax-free. This applies whether they've fully retired, reached age 65, or are drawing a transition to retirement (TTR) income stream while still working.

The nuance worth knowing: turning 60 makes payments to the member tax-free, but it doesn't automatically make the fund's investment earnings tax-free. A TTR pension for a client who hasn't met a full condition of release (e.g. still working, under 65) is still taxed on earnings up to 15% inside the fund, like accumulation phase — the tax-free earnings treatment is a separate step, covered below.

3. Fully retired — the retirement phase

Once a client meets a full condition of release — genuine retirement, or simply turning 65 — and moves their balance into an account-based retirement phase pension, investment earnings inside that pension become tax-free, up to the transfer balance cap. Combined with tax-free payments from age 60, this is the most tax-effective phase super offers.

Earnings inside the pension

0% tax on investment earnings within the retirement phase account, up to the transfer balance cap — $2.1 million from 2026–27. Any amount above the cap generally needs to stay in, or be moved back to, accumulation phase, where the usual up-to-15% earnings tax applies.

Payments to the client

Pension payments and lump sum withdrawals remain tax-free from age 60, exactly as in stage 2 — retirement phase changes the tax on earnings inside the fund, not the tax on what the client receives.

How we say it to a client

"Three things change over time, not just one: how your contributions are taxed, how the growth is taxed, and how withdrawals are taxed. By the time you're fully retired, two of those three drop to zero — that's the whole point of the structure."

11 — SMSF

Everything self-managed super in one place — what a trustee can and can't do, the compliance basics, and the law changes that just landed.

What an SMSF actually is

A self-managed super fund is a super fund a client runs themselves, as trustee, instead of an APRA-regulated fund running it for them. That single fact — the client is the trustee — is the source of almost everything else on this page. Every decision, and every compliance failure, sits with them (or with the corporate trustee they control), not with a fund manager in the background.

Trustee duties — the compliance essentials

Sole purpose test

The fund must be run for the sole purpose of providing retirement benefits — not for any current-day personal benefit to a member or their relatives. This single test underpins most of the "can't do" list below.

Investment strategy

Trustees must prepare and maintain a written investment strategy, covering diversification, liquidity, risk, and insurance for members — and actually invest in line with it.

Annual audit and return

Every SMSF needs an independent approved auditor each year, and must lodge an SMSF annual return with the ATO — this covers both super regulatory compliance and the fund's income tax.

Pension calculations

If the fund is paying a pension, the trustee is responsible for correctly calculating and paying the minimum (and, for a TTR, the maximum) each year — see Tab 05 for the current percentages.

14 — Factual info vs advice

The most important line on any call. Read this before you rely on anything else in this handbook out loud to a client.

Three tiers, one safe zone

Australian financial services law recognises three tiers of information. Only one of them is safe for this team to give without a licensed adviser involved.

TierWhat it isCan this team give it?
Factual informationObjective, verifiable facts — rates, rules, definitions, how something works mechanically. No opinion, no recommendation, nothing tailored to the person.Yes — this is the safe zone
General adviceA recommendation or opinion about a product or strategy that doesn't take personal circumstances into account — even generic, non-tailored suggestions. Legally still "advice," and still requires an AFSL authorisation to give.No — requires a licence this team doesn't hold
Personal adviceAdvice that considers, or could reasonably be seen to consider, the client's own objectives, financial situation or needs. Requires the full advice process — fact find, best interests duty, Statement of Advice.No — requires a licensed adviser and the full advice process
The team on these calls is not authorised to give general advice, only personal advice authorised advisers can — which means the usual "just don't get personal, stick to general advice" rule of thumb doesn't apply here. The safe zone is narrower: factual information only. Even a generic, non-personalised suggestion or opinion ("that type of cover tends to suit people in that situation") is general advice, and it's outside what this team can say.

The test to run in your head

Before answering, ask: am I about to state a fact, or am I about to suggest, recommend, or give an opinion on what they should do?

Safe — stating a fact

"The concessional cap this year is $32,500." "Super is generally tax-free to withdraw from age 60." "TPD stands for total and permanent disability cover." These are true regardless of who's asking.

Not safe — advice

"You should salary sacrifice more." "I'd increase your income protection." "It makes sense for you to combine your accounts." Anything that answers "what should I do" has crossed into advice.

Red-flag phrases

If a client says any of these, they're asking for advice, not information — regardless of how the rest of the sentence is worded.

"What should I do…" · "Is it worth it for me…" · "What would you do…" · "Should I switch / combine / increase / take out…" · "Is my situation okay…" · "Am I on track…" · "Is X enough for me…"

The safe response to any of these is the same shape every time: answer the factual part if there is one, then redirect the "what should I do" part to a licensed adviser.

Redirect script

"I can absolutely explain how that works — [give the factual answer]. Whether that's the right move for you specifically is exactly the kind of thing our licensed advisers work through with clients, so let's get that booked in rather than me guessing at it now."
